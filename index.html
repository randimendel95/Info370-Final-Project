Introduction¶

Of all criminal incidents reported to the National Incident-Based Reporting System (NIBRS), 3.3% took place on a college campus. College is an important space where students are given the opportunity for higher knowledge and learning as they transition to a more independent state. However, crime at such institutions is an issue for students and parents alike, as well as officials who work to mitigate such crimes. Colleges invest significant amounts of money in preventing crime, but data relevant to campus crime is often only reported as a historical, after-the-fact statistics. Being able to use data about colleges as a predictor of criminal offenses could help college officials develop plans to reduce crime rates. Through our study we hope to provide officials with this data, and also offer it to students as it may impact their decision on which school to attend.

Introduction to Dataset¶

Our research question was how can different information about a college allow us to predict crime rate for a college? We found two datasets that we combined in order to get a holistic view of school information compared to crime statistics for that school.

  1. Campus safety and security dataset, including number of criminal offense occurrences in each school in 2014.
  2. College Scorecard dataset, was consist of a wide range of data element about a school (average cost of attendance, average faculty salary, etc.).

The original College Scorecard dataset included a large number of data elements about each school. Some data in the dataset was unavailable due to school privacy policy. After filtering out irrelevant data and null values and conducting an inner join of the two datasets, the final dataset was consist of 5,791 rows and 8 columns. When using the crime data we normalized it by divided number of criminal incidents by population of the college and multiplied it by 1000 to get crimes per 1000 students.

Introduction to K-Nearest-Neighbors (KNN)¶

K-nearest-neighbors (KNN) is a robust algorithm used to classify data. KNN falls in the supervised learning family of algorithms. In order to use KNN, we are given a labelled dataset consisting of training observations (x, y) and would like to capture the relationship between x and y. In this example we will use x to denote a feature (i.e., an attribute, what we are using to predict) and y to denote the outcome we are trying to predict.

In the classification setting, the K-nearest neighbor algorithm essentially boils down to forming a majority vote between the K most similar instances to a given unseen observation. One of the pros of using a KNN model is that it can learn complex decision boundaries on the fly. However, it is worth noting that the minimal training phase of KNN comes both at a memory cost, since we must store a potentially huge data set, as well as a computational cost during test time since classifying a given observation requires a run down of the whole data set.

Apply KNN to Our Dataset¶

Now that we have explained KNN is is important to explain how that links with our data. We ended up with 5,791 colleges which were used to create our model. We split this data into 80% training, and 20% test data, then ran 10-fold cross validation using a k nearest neighbors algorithm to find the colleges that had the most similar attributes to a particular college in question, and tested this with multiple values of the most similar colleges, by averaging their crime rates. We used 5 features to measure similarity between colleges: percentage of financially independent students, percent of first generation college students, average family income, control of the institution(private or public), and the predominant degree achieved by students. Once we normalized these features from the minimum value to the maximum on a scale of 0 to 1, we used our algorithm to find the sum of distances of each feature between each college in the training set to our test value, in order to find the values with the least difference in feature to the college in question. We ran this using several different numbers of neighbors, and found the root mean squared error in our predictions for each test set in our 10 folds, and found the average error in our predictions, choosing the number of compared neighbors with the least error in prediction.

As shown below, somewhere around 250 neighbors would be our ideal K neighbors, however this model may not be accurate since this large number means that it is likely that neighbors with similar attributes don't actually tend to have the same crime rates, based on our given data, with only the given attributes weighted equally.

Limitations¶

Our dataset had some inherent limitations, the greatest of which is the fact that it relies on reports from different sources. In some cases crime incident reports may have come from schools self-reporting, while in others they may have come from local law enforcement. In some instances schools did not report data. Additionally, schools are located in different states and local jurisdictions which may have different definitions of criminal offences. For instance, substances which may result in a drug-related criminal arrest at colleges located in one state may be legal in others.

Our dataset was also relatively small (~5,000 rows) for the type of predicting we aimed to do. We may have been able to predict more accurately with additional data. Lastly, we only analyzed five different features to predict crime rate. We therefore acknowledge that the predictions we made may not be accurate as there are many other factors that come into play.

Discussion¶

Using our model we built we can now predict a colleges crime rate based on the five variables we used. This information could be used by students in high school looking to avoid schools who may have a higher crime rate. We chose to focus, for this first iteration of our project, on total crimes, cumulated for a college, however this could be extended to more common crimes such as liquor law violations, or more serious and violent crimes alike in order to predict which crimes may be present at a particular college, if these results may differ more with different features of a school.
